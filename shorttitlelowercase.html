    1.

    Prove that if \(a \equiv b\pmod{n}\) and \(c\equiv d\pmod{n}\text{,}\) then \(ac\equiv bd\pmod{n}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    If \(a \equiv b \pmod{n}\text{,}\) then \(n | (a-b)\text{,}\) write \(nk = a-b\text{.}\) If \(c \equiv d \pmod{n}\text{,}\) then \(n | (c-d)\text{,}\) write \(nm = c-d\text{.}\) Thus, \(ac = (b+nk)(d+nm) = bd+bnm+dnk+n^2mk\)\(= bd+n(mb+dk+nmk)\text{.}\) Therefore, \(n|ac-bd\text{,}\) showing that \(ac \equiv bd \pmod{n}\text{.}\)

    2.

    Prove that if \(n>2\text{,}\) then there exists a prime \(p\) satisfying \(n<p<n!\text{.}\) (Hint: Consider the number \(n!-1\text{.}\))
    🔗
    Solution.
    \(n!-1<n!\) always holds and since \(n>2\text{,}\) \(n! \ge 2n > n+1\) so that \(n!-1>n\text{.}\) In particular, \(n<n!-1<n!\) for all \(n>2\text{.}\) If \(n!-1\) is prime, we are done. If not, then by the FToA it has a prime factorization and each of those primes must be greater than \(n\) (or it would divide \(n!\) and \(n!-1\text{,}\) an impossibility). Those primes must also be less than \(n!\) because they divide \(n!-1\text{.}\) Hence each of those primes lie between \(n\) and \(n!\text{.}\)
    🔗

    3.

    Show your Euclidean Algorithm skills by using it to find \(\gcd(63,23)\text{.}\) Then use the algorithm to write \(\gcd(63,23)\) as a linear combination of 63 and 23.
    🔗
    Solution.
    \(63 = 2\cdot 23 + 17\)
    🔗
    \(23 = 1\cdot 17 + 6\)
    🔗
    \(17 = 2\cdot 6 + 5\)
    🔗
    \(6 = 1\cdot 5 + 1\)
    🔗
    \(5 = 5\cdot 1 + 0\)
    🔗
    Thus \(\gcd(63,23)=1\text{.}\) Back‑substituting gives
    🔗
    \(1 = 11\cdot 23 - 4\cdot 63\)
    🔗

    4.

    Let \(n = p_1^{e_1} p_2^{e_2} \cdots p_k^{e_k}\) be the prime factorization of \(n\text{.}\) How many positive divisors does \(n\) have? Explain your answer.
    🔗
    Solution.
    By Theorem 2.12, a divisor of \(n\) must be of the form \(p_1^{f_1} p_2^{f_2} \cdots p_k^{f_k}\text{,}\) where \(0 \le f_i \le e_i\) for each \(i=1,2,\dots,k\text{.}\) There are \(e_i+1\) choices for each \(f_i\text{.}\) Each of these divisors is unique because they have a distinct prime factorization. Therefore, the total number of divisors is \((e_1+1)(e_2+1)\cdots(e_k+1)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    5.

    Prove that if \(\gcd(a,b) = 1\) then \(\gcd(a+b,ab) = 1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    (Prime solution:) If \(ab = 1\) we are done because \(\gcd(a+b,ab) = \gcd(a+b,1) = 1\text{.}\) If \(ab \ne 1\text{,}\) let \(p\) be a prime dividing \(ab\text{.}\) By Theorem 2.27, \(p|a\) or \(p|b\text{.}\) Note that \(p\) cannot divide both \(a\) and \(b\) since \(\gcd(a,b)=1\text{.}\) In particular, \(p \nmid a+b\text{.}\) Therefore \(p\) is not a prime divisor of \(\gcd(a+b,ab)\text{.}\) Since no prime dividing \(ab\) divides \(a+b\text{,}\) we have \(\gcd(a+b,ab) = 1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (Non-prime way:) \(\gcd(a+b,a) = 1\) because any common divisor of \(a\) and \(a+b\) must divide \(b\text{.}\) Similarly, \(\gcd(a+b,b) = 1\text{.}\) Therefore, by Theorem 2.28, \(\gcd(a+b,ab) = \gcd(a+b,a)\cdot \gcd(a+b,b) = 1\cdot 1 = 1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    6.

    Show that if \(n\) is composite, then \(n\) has a prime divisor less than or equal to \(\sqrt{n}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Write \(n=ab\) with integers \(1<a\le b\text{.}\) Then \(a^2\le n\text{,}\) so \(a\le\sqrt{n}\text{.}\) Any prime divisor \(p\) of \(a\) satisfies \(p\le a\le\sqrt{n}\) and since \(a\) divides \(n\text{,}\) \(p\) also divides \(n\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    7.

    Prove that there are infinitely many prime numbers. (You may use Euclid’s proof.)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Assume there are finitely many primes, \(p_1, p_2, \dots, p_k\text{.}\) Let \(N = p_1 p_2 \cdots p_k + 1\text{.}\) Since 2 is a prime the list of primes is not empty and \(N > 1\text{,}\) \(N\) must be divisible by some prime by Theorem 2.1. But \(N\) is not divisible by any of \(p_1, p_2, \dots, p_k\text{,}\) because dividing \(N\) by any of these gives a remainder of 1. Therefore, \(N\) must be divisible by a prime not in the list \(p_1, p_2, \dots, p_k\text{,}\) contradicting the assumption that we have a complete list of primes. Hence, there must be infinitely many primes.
    🔗

    8.

    Find the canonical residue of \(19^{53} \pmod{11}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    \(19 \equiv 8 \pmod{11}\text{,}\) \(19^2 = 64 \equiv -2 \pmod{11}\text{,}\) \(19^4 \equiv 4 \pmod{11}\text{,}\) \(19^8 \equiv 5 \pmod{11}\text{,}\) \(19^{16} \equiv 3 \pmod{11}\text{,}\) \(19^{32} \equiv 9 \pmod{11}\text{.}\) Since \(53 = 32+16+4+1\text{,}\) \(19^{53} = 19^{32} 19^{16} 19^{4} 19^{1} \equiv 9 \cdot 3 \cdot 4 \cdot 8 \equiv 6 \pmod{11}\text{.}\)
